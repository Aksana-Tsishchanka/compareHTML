Individuals can deduct the amount of unreimbursed I.R.C. §213(a)medical expenses that exceeds a threshold percentage of their adjusted gross income (AGI). In order to qualify for the deduction, the expenses must be paid for medical care, which includes payments for medical or dental services, prescribed medicine and drugs, insulin, medical equipment and supplies, medical insurance premiums, transportation needed to get medical care, and many other medical-related expenses.

The medical expense deduction is an itemized deduction and is reported on Schedule A (Form 1040), Itemized Deductions. Neither Form 1040A nor Form 1040EZ can be used to claim a medical expense deduction.

Deduction requirements. Individuals can claim a I.R.C. §213(a)deduction for medical expenses:

(1)

That are paid during the tax year and are not compensated for by insurance or otherwise;

(2)

That are for the medical care of the individual, the individual’s spouse, or the individual’s qualifying dependent; and

(3)

To the extent that the expenses exceed 10 percent of adjusted gross income (AGI).

Senior citizens can claim a medical expense deduction to the extent that their qualifying expenses exceed 7.5 percent of AGI. The 7.5-percent AGI threshold for senior citizens is temporary and only applies for tax years ending before 2017. For tax years beginning in 2011 and 2012, the percentage of AGI threshold was 7.5 percent for all individuals, no matter what their age.

There are a few exceptions to the rule that medical expenses are deductible in the year paid.

(1)

An individual may deduct qualified premiums paid for future medical care to be provided after the individual reaches the age of 65.

(2)

Certain prepayments for lifetime institutional care are deductible.

(3)

The medical expenses of a decedent paid by the decedent's estate within one year of the decedent's death can be treated as paid by the decedent in the year the expenses were incurred.

Recordkeeping. Individuals need to keep records of their medical and dental expenses, but they do not need to submit them with their return in order to claim the medical expense deduction. Individuals claiming medical expense deductions should be prepared to provide the name and address of each person who received payment for medical expenses and the amount and date of payment.

Medical expense deductions must be substantiated by Treas. Reg. §1.213-1(h)statements or itemized invoices from the doctor, hospital, pharmacy or other person or entity who was paid for the medical service. The statement or invoice should show:

  • the nature of the service provided and the person who received the service ;

  • the nature of any other item of expense and for whom the expense was incurred, for what specific purpose the expense was incurred, the amount that was paid, and the date of payment; and

  • any other relevant information.

Common Examples of Deductible and Nondeductible Medical Expenses

In order to be eligible for the medical expense deduction, the individual’s expenses must be paid for medical care, which includes payments for medical or dental services, prescribed medicine and drugs, insulin, medical equipment and supplies, medical insurance premiums, transportation needed to get medical care, and many other medical-related expenses. Although capital expenditures are generally not deductible, if the purpose of a capital expenditure is for the medical care of an individual or the individual’s spouse or dependents, it can be deductible.

The key issue for the medical expense deduction is what expenses are considered to be paid for medical care. "I.R.C. §213(d)(1)Medical care" is defined as amounts paid for the diagnosis, cure, mitigation, treatment, or prevention of disease, or for the purpose of affecting any structure or function of the body. Medical care also includes amounts paid for transportation to get medical care, premiums paid for insurance that covers the cost of medical care, amounts paid for long-term care services, and limited amounts of premiums paid for a qualified long-term care insurance contract. In addition, a limited amount of I.R.C. §213(d)(2)lodging costs that are necessary for medical care may qualify as medical care expenses.

Reporting Medical Expense Deduction on Schedule A (Form 1040), Itemized Deductions

The medical expense deduction is reported on Lines 1 through 4 of Schedule A (Form 1040), Itemized Deductions (2014). Medical expenses after reductions for reimbursements are reported on Line 1. The taxpayer's AGI is reported on Line 2. The AGI limitation is computed on Line 3 by multiplying the taxpayer's AGI from Line 2 by 10 percent (or 7.5 percent for those born before January 2, 1950, e.g., 65 or over in 2014). The taxpayer's medical expense deduction for the tax year is the amount computed on Line 4, which is equal to the medical expenses from Line 1 less the AGI limitation from Line 3.

Example 

Peter and Ellen, who are both under 65 years old in 2014, file as married jointly. For the year, they have an adjusted gross income of $60,000. Their reportable medical expenses for the year (as detailed below) are $9,300. Their unusually high medical expenses for the year were in part attributable to a back injury that Peter, an aspiring tennis pro, suffered while playing in a tennis match. Their qualifying medical expenses are follows:

(1)

$4,240 in doctor bills and $200 in prescribed medicine and drugs for Ellen's mother, who is a dependent;

(2)

$400 in prescribed medicine and drugs for Peter's back injury;

(3)

$900 was paid for the combined family medical and dental insurance;

(4)

$3,400, representing Peter's total hospital and doctor bills for his injury of $5,900, less $2,500 that was reimbursed by his insurance company;

(5)

the $45 expense of transporting Peter to and from the hospital and the doctor's office; and

(6)

the $115 cost of new eyeglasses for Ellen.

Peter and Ellen report $9,300 in medical expenses in 2014 ($4,240 + $200 + $400 + $900 + $3,400 + $45 + $115). This amount is amount is reduced by $6,000 (10 percent of their adjusted gross income of $60,000), resulting in $3,300 in medical deductions to be reported as itemized deductions. In addition, Peter and Ellen paid $5,600 in real estate taxes, $7,200 in mortgage interest expense, and made $1,700 in charitable donations, for total itemized deductions of $17,800. This amount is transferred to Line 40 of Form 1040.
