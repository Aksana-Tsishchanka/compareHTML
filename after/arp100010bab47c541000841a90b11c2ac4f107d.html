Code Explanations,Determining Recovery Period for an MACRS Asset Using Rev. Proc. 87-56

IRC
CCH Explanations
Tools

In order to compute depreciation under the Modified Accelerated Cost Recovery System (MACRS), it is necessary to first determine the asset's MACRS property class. The asset's recovery period (i.e., its depreciation period), applicable depreciation method, and applicable convention depend on the asset's property class.

Under MACRS, an asset's property class is based on either the type of asset or the business activity in which the asset is primarily used.

MACRS provides ten property classes and assigns a recovery period for each property class. If the alternative depreciation system (ADS) applies to the property class, however, a longer recovery period is usually assigned.

The key resource for determining an asset's property class is the asset classification table contained in Rev. Proc. 87-56Rev. Proc. 87-56. This table is reproduced and updated annually in IRS Pub. 946IRS Publication 946 (How to Depreciation Property). Class Lives and Recovery Period

The first section of the table lists assets commonly used in many businesses. These assets include computers and related equipment, office furniture and equipment, noncommercial airplanes, automobiles, taxis, light and heavy trucks, truck trailers, vessels, barges, tugs, and land improvements.

The second section of the table contains a comprehensive list of business activities and, in some cases, a description of assets that are peculiar to those business activities.

Each section of the table provides the regular MACRS recovery period (i.e., the MACRS general depreciation system (GDS) recovery period) and the MACRS alternative depreciation system (ADS) recovery period for the asset or business activity that falls within an asset class. The ADS depreciation system, which applies if an election is made or in certain situations is mandatory, uses a depreciation period that is usually longer than the GDS depreciation period.

If the asset is not listed within an asset class in the first section of the table, then the applicable recovery period is the one provided in the second section of the table for asset classes relating to the business activity in which the asset is used.

The "Identifying MACRS Class Life" tool may be used to help determine the asset class, class life, regular recovery period, and ADS recovery period of an asset.

Example 

An accountant buys a desk for business use. The desk is specifically described in the first part of the table under Asset Class 00.11, Office Furniture, Fixtures, and Equipment. Under Asset Class 00.11 the desk is treated as 7-year property.

Example 

An accountant purchases a depreciable reference library. Books are not listed in the first section of the classification table. The MACRS recovery period is, therefore, based on the business activity of the accountant contained in the second section of the table. An accountant's business activity is described in Asset Class 57.0 (relating to assets used in personal and professional services) which provides a 5-year GDS recovery period and an 8-year ADS recovery period. Since the GDS period is 5 years, the asset is considered MACRS 5-year property.

A land improvement may be listed in both sections of the table. This rarely happens but when it does the recovery period provided in the second section of the table for the land improvement is used even through it is described in the first section of the table.

In some cases, an asset or the business activity in which it is used is not listed in either section of the table. By default, these assets are treated as MACRS 7-year property and have a regular (GDS) 7-year recovery period. If such an asset is personal property, its ADS recovery period is 12 years. If the asset is section 1245 real property, a 40 year ADS recovery period is assigned.

Nonresidential real property has a 39 year recovery period regardless of the business activity it is used in. Residential rental property always has a 27.5-year recovery period.

Asset Used in More Than One Business Activity at Same Time

Occasionally an asset is used in two or more business activities at the same time. In this case, the property class is based on the business activity in which the asset is primarily used. If the business activity in which an asset is used changes permanently, then a special depreciation computation under the MACRS change in use rules may be required.

Depreciation Period for Leased Property

Leased property may be depreciated by the lessor under MACRS if the lease is bona fide. If the lease is in effect a Rev. Rul. 55-540sales contract, the lessee is entitled to the depreciation deductions.

If the lessor is entitled to the deductions, the following rules apply in determining the proper MACRS depreciation period.

(1)

If the leased asset is described in the first part of the Asset Class table (e.g. a car), use the depreciation period prescribed for such assets;

(2)

If the asset is not described in the first part of the table and the business activity of leasing the assets is described in the second part of the table, use the depreciation period prescribed for the leasing activity;

(3)

If the neither (1) nor (2) apply, use the depreciation period prescribed by the second part of the table for the business activity of the lessee;

(4)

If neither (1), (2), or (3) apply, the leased asset is classified as 7-year MACRS property under the default rule that applies to assets without an assigned depreciation period.

The table does not specifically prescribe the business activity of leasing. However, the IRS has interpreted Asset Class 57.0 of the table as applying to the activity of leasing residential real estate for lessees for personal use. Asset Class 57.0 applies to assets used in wholesale and retail trade, and personal and professional services. Apparently, the IRS believes that the rental of residential real property relates to the provision of personal services. The same logic may apply to the rental of commercial property at least if the lessor remains responsible for maintaining the property.

Special rules apply to I.R.C. §168(e)(3)(A)(iii)consumer durables subject to rent-to-own leases. A 3-year recovery period generally applies to such property. See ¶168(e)-child1Explanation: §168(e).
