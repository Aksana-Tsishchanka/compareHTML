Code Explanations,Related Entities

IRC
CCH Explanations

A lease may be characterized as a lease of tax-exempt use property if a party that is related to a tax-exempt organization is involved. Related parties can include governmental entities or nongovernmental entities.

Governmental Units

Each governmental unit and each agency or instrumentality of a governmental unit is related to each other if the units, agencies, or instrumentalities directly or indirectly derive their powers, rights, and duties (in whole or in part) from the I.R.C. §168(h)(4)(A)(i)same sovereign authority. The United States, each state, and each possession of the United States is treated as a I.R.C. §168(h)(4)(A)(ii) separate sovereign authority.

For example, a city located in one state is not related to a city located in another state because each state is deemed to have separate sovereign authority. Conversely, each city within a single state is related to all other cities in that state. If ten cities in one state each own a 10-percent interest in a corporation, the state, each city, and the corporation are all considered I.R.C. §168(h)(4)(A)(i)related parties.

Nongovernmental Units

For entities that are not governmental units, related party status is determined with reference to the organizations' purposes and control. When two entities have significant common purposes and substantial common membership, or if they are subject to direct or indirect substantial common direction or control, they are deemed to be I.R.C. §168(h)(4)(B)related entities.

In addition, a tax-exempt entity is related to any other entity if either entity owns, directly or indirectly, a I.R.C. §168(h)(4)(C)(i)50-percent or greater interest in the capital or profits of the other.

Finally, an organization or entity is considered to be related to another with respect to a transaction if that transaction is part of an attempt by the entities to avoid the application of the tax-exempt entity leasing rules I.R.C. §168(h)(4)(D)avoid the application of the tax-exempt entity leasing rules.

Like-Kind Exchanges of Tax-Exempt Use Property by Related Entities

A taxpayer that receives depreciable property for tax-exempt use property in a like-kind exchange involving a related party must depreciate the property received as though it was the exchanged tax-exempt use property if a principal purpose of the transaction is to avoid application of the Treas. Reg. §1.168(h)-1MACRS alternative depreciation system (ADS).

The depreciable property (tainted property) is treated in the same manner as the exchanged property (related tax-exempt use property) for purposes of determining the allowable depreciation deduction. Thus, the Treas. Reg. §1.168(h)-1(b)tainted property is depreciated by the taxpayer over the remaining recovery period of, and using the same depreciation method and convention as, the related tax-exempt use property

Example 

Black Corporation owns all of the stock of two subsidiaries, B corporation and S corporation. Black Corporation and its two subsidiaries do not file a consolidated federal income tax return. On May 5, 2010, B corporation purchases aircraft I for $1 million and leases it to a foreign airline whose income is not subject to U.S. taxation and which is a tax-exempt entity. On the same date, S corporation owns an aircraft (aircraft II) with a fair market value of $1 million, which is leased to an airline that is a U.S. taxpayer. S corporation's adjusted basis in aircraft II is $0. The next day, at a time when each aircraft is still worth $1 million B corporation transfers aircraft I to S corporation (subject to the lease to the foreign airline) in exchange for aircraft II (subject to the lease to the airline that is a U.S. taxpayer). S corporation realizes gain of $1 million on the exchange, but that gain is not recognized because the exchange is of like-kind properties. Following the exchange, S corporation has a $0 basis in aircraft I pursuant to the like-kind exchange rules. B corporation has a $1 million basis in aircraft II. If a principal purpose of the transfer is to avoid the application of the alternative depreciation system, B must recover its $1 million basis in aircraft II over the remaining recovery period of, and using the same depreciation method and convention as that of aircraft I, Treas. Reg. §1.168(h)-1(d)the related tax-exempt use property.

Tainted Property Rule

The Treas. Reg. §1.168(h)-1(b)(2)tainted property rule generally applies only to the portion of the taxpayer's basis in the tainted property that does not exceed the taxpayer's adjusted basis in the related tax-exempt use property prior to the transfer.

In addition, the rule does not apply to the portion of the basis in tainted property received in a nontaxable transaction, including:

  • distributions in complete liquidation in a controlled subsidiary;

  • transfers to corporations controlled by the transferor;

  • exchanges under tax-free reorganizations;

  • transfers in exchange for an interest in a partnership; and

  • distributions to a partner and transactions between members of the same affiliated group when the group files a consolidated return.

In these cases, the property is already bound to be depreciated under the methods used by the transferor in the nontaxable transaction.

Example 

First Corporation owns all of the stock of two subsidiaries, B corporation and Z corporation. B corporation and Z corporation are members of an affiliated group that files a consolidated federal income tax return. B corporation and Z corporation each own identical aircraft. B corporation's aircraft (FA) is leased to a tax-exempt entity and has a fair market value of $1 million and an adjusted basis of $500,000. Z corporation's aircraft (DA) is leased to a U.S. taxpayer and has a fair market value of $1 million and an adjusted basis of $10,000. On May 1, 2002, the two subsidiaries exchange aircraft, subject to their respective leases. If a principal purpose of the transfer is to avoid the application of the alternative depreciation system, B corporation must depreciate $490,000 of the basis of DA over the remaining recovery period of FA, using the same depreciation method and convention. However, the remaining $10,000 must be depreciated using the transferor's recovery period and depreciation method for DA, under the rules for nontaxable transactions between affiliated groups that file a consolidated return.
