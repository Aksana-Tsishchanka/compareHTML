Code Explanations,Limits on Tax When Section 481(a) Adjustments for an Accounting Change Are Substantial

IRC
Regulations

Taxpayers who change accounting methods must compute a section 481(a) adjustment to prevent amounts from being duplicated or omitted from taxable income due to the change. For a discussion on how to compute a section 481(a) adjustment, see ¶481(a)What Are Required Section 481(a) Adjustments for an Accounting Method Change?. If a net adjustment increases taxable income for the year of change by more than $3,000, the taxpayer can use one of I.R.C. §481(b)two allocation methods to limit the increase in tax from the section 481(a) adjustment.

The two allocation methods for limiting the increase in tax are:

(1)

a three-year allocation method, or

(2)

an allocation method based on the new accounting method.

The two allocation methods affect the amount of the resulting tax, not the time for inclusion of the section 481(a) adjustment. A taxpayer should consider the two methods for limiting tax regardless of whether the entire adjustment must be included in taxable income for the year of change or can be spread over future years. For a discussion of section 481(a) adjustment periods, see ¶481(c)How Are Section 481(a) Adjustments for an Accounting Change Taken into Account in Computing Tax?

If both methods of limiting the tax can be applied, then the increase in tax for the year of change is the smallest of the increases in tax resulting from including the section 481(a) adjustments: (1) entirely in the year of the change, (2) according to the three-year allocation method, or (3) according to an allocation based on the new accounting method. The Treas. Reg. §1.481-2(c)(1)increase in tax for the year of change is equal to the excess of the tax that would result from taking the required adjustments into account in the year of change over the tax for that year without taking the required adjustments into account.

Three-Year Allocation Method for Limiting Tax from Section 481(a) Adjustment

Taxpayers can use the I.R.C. §481(b)(1)three-year allocation method for limiting tax from an accounting method change if:

(1)

the old method of accounting was used in the two tax years before the year of change; and

(2)

the net amount of the required adjustments increases taxable income for the year of change by more than $3,000.

Taxpayers using the three-year allocation method allocate the net amount of the section 481(a) adjustments ratably over a three-year period that includes the year of change and the two preceding tax years. The taxpayer allocates the adjustments evenly over the three-year period regardless of whether the adjustments are in fact attributable to such years.

The taxpayer then computes the tax increases that would result from allocating the adjustments ratably over the three-year period. The increase in tax for the year of the change is Treas. Reg. §1.481-2(a)limited to the sum of the increases in tax for the three years that results from a one-third allocation of the adjustments to each year. For example, under the three-year allocation method, the tax increase due to $15,000 of adjustments is limited to the sum of the increases in tax for the year of change and the two preceding tax years that would result from including an additional $5,000 in income in each of those years.

Thus, under the Treas. Reg. §1.481-2(A)three-year allocation method, the tax for the year of change is the lesser of:

(1)

the tax attributable to taking the adjustment into account in computing taxable income for the year of change; or

(2)

the sum of the increases in tax that would result if the adjustments were included ratably in the year of change and the two preceding tax years.

Example 

In 2015, Acme Business is changing a method of accounting that it used in 2013 and 2014. The net amount of section 481(a) adjustments increases taxable income for 2015 by $3,300. Under the three-year allocation method, the increase in tax for 2015 cannot be more than the sum of the increases in tax for 2015, 2014, and 2013 that would have resulted from the inclusion of $1,100 in income in each of those years.

Taxpayer that can establish the amount of taxable income using the new accounting method for one or more consecutive years before the year of change should compute their tax under using the new method for those years to see if it is less than the tax computed using the three-year allocation method. See "Allocation Method for Limiting Tax from Section 481(a) Adjustment Based on New Accounting Method," below.

Allocation Method for Limiting Tax from Section 481(a) Adjustment Based on New Accounting Method

Taxpayers can limit the tax from an accounting method change by using an allocation method based on the I.R.C. §481(b)(2)new accounting method if:

(1)

the taxpayer can establish, through books of account and other records, what taxable income would have been under the new accounting method for one or more consecutive years immediately preceding the year of change; and

(2)

the net amount of the required adjustments increases taxable income for the year of change by more than $3,000.

Under this allocation method, the taxpayer computes the tax increases that would result if the necessary section 481(a) adjustments were allocated back to the preceding years based on the new method. Adjustments that cannot be allocated to prior years are included in the income for the year of the change.

Example 

Acme Business is changing its method of accounting in 2015 and has used the old method in 2013 and 2014. The net amount of adjustments increases taxable income for 2015 by $3,300. Acme Business is able to establish taxable income using the new method of accounting for the years 2012 – 2015.

Of the total $3,300 adjustment, $900 is attributable to 2015, the year of change. The remaining portion of the adjustment can be allocated back to the preceding years using the new accounting method as follows: 2014, $1,200; 2013, ($300)*; 2012, $1,500. Using the allocation method based on the new accounting method, the increase in tax from the inclusion of the total adjustments in 2015 is limited to the net increase in tax that would result by increasing taxable income for 2012, 2014, and 2015 by $1,500, $1,200 and $900, respectively, and by decreasing taxable income for 2013 by $300.

Taxpayers who can make an allocation based on the new accounting method should also compute tax using the three-year allocation method to see which method results in the lower amount of tax. See "Three-Year Allocation Method for Limiting Tax from Section 481(a) Adjustment," above. The increase in tax for the year of change is the smallest of the increases in tax resulting from including adjustments: (1) entirely in the year of the change, (2) according to the three-year allocation method, or (3) according to an allocation based on the new accounting method.

Tax from Section 481(a) Adjustment When Both Three-Year Allocation and Allocation Based on New Accounting Method Apply

When a taxpayer can apply both the three-year allocation method and an allocation based on the new accounting method to limit the tax for the year of an accounting method change, the increase in tax from the required section 481(a) adjustment is the smallest of:

(1)

the tax attributable to the adjustments for the tax year of change;

(2)

the tax attributable to the adjustments, as computed under the three-year allocation method; or

(3)

the net increase in taxes that would result from allocating part of the adjustments to one or more consecutive preceding tax years that can be determined under the new method of accounting and the balance to the year of change.

A taxpayer should complete the following steps to determine the tax attributable to a net adjustment when both methods can be applied.

(1)

Compute the increase in tax for the year of change attributable to the amount by which taxable income for that year is increased solely because of the adjustment. (Tax for tax year computed with net adjustment − Tax for tax year computed without net adjustment = Treas. Reg. §1.481-2(C)(1)Tax attributable to increase due to net adjustment).

(2)

Compute the tax attributable to the adjustment for the year of change and for the two preceding tax years using the three-year allocation method by treating one-third of the net amount of the adjustment as received or accrued in each of those tax years. (Tax for tax year computed with one-third of net adjustment − Tax for tax year computed without one-third of net adjustment = Tax attributable to increase due to one-third of net adjustment). The taxpayer must account for any tax year preceding the year of change to which no adjustment is allocated but which is affected by an NOL or by a capital loss carryback or carryover.

(3)

If the taxpayer meets the requirements for using an Treas. Reg. §1.481-2(C)(3)allocation based on the new accounting method:

(a)

Calculate the amount of the adjustment allocated to the year of change and to the one or more preceding consecutive tax years established under the new method by allocating to each preceding year an amount equal to any increase in taxable income for that preceding year as computed without regard to any adjustments attributable to that year. If the increase in taxable income for any such year is less than the amount of the adjustment, the increase is deemed to equal the amount of the adjustment remaining to be allocated;

(b)

Determine the amount of the net increase in tax attributable to the adjustments for the year of change, the consecutive tax year or years immediately preceding the year of change for which the taxpayer can establish taxable income under the new method of accounting, and any tax year preceding the year of change to which no adjustment is allocated but which is affected by an NOL or by a capital loss carryback or carryover. (Tax for each year computed with allocable portion of adjustment − Tax for each year computed without any portion of adjustment).

(4)

Calculate the tax for the year of change without taking any adjustments into account.

(5)

Increase the tax computed for the year of change by the smallest of the following:

(a)

The amount of tax for the year of change attributable solely to the entire amount of the adjustments;

(b)

The sum of the increases in tax liability for the year of the change plus the two immediately preceding tax years, as computed under the three-year allocation method in (2), above; or

(c)

The net increase in tax attributable to allocating the adjustments under the new method of accounting rule, as computed in (3), above.

Example 

Acme Business is changing its method of accounting in 2015 and has used the old method in 2013 and 2014. The net amount of adjustments increases taxable income for 2015 by $3,300. Under the three-year allocation method, the increase in tax for 2015 cannot be more than the sum of the increases in tax for 2015, 2014, and 2013 that would have resulted from the inclusion of $1,100 in income in each of those years.

Acme Business is also able to establish the new method of accounting for the years 2012 – 2015. Of the total $3,300 adjustment, $900 is attributable to 2015, the year of change. The remaining portion of the adjustment can be allocated back to the preceding years using the new accounting method as follows: 2014, $1,200; 2013, ($300)*; 2012, $1,500. The increase in tax from the inclusion of the total adjustments in 2015 is limited to the net increase in tax that would result by increasing taxable income for 2012, 2014, and 2015 by $1,500, $1,200 and $900, respectively, and by decreasing taxable income for 2013 by $300.

The increase in tax for 2015 is the smallest of the increases in tax resulting from: (1) the inclusion of the $3,300 adjustment in income in 2015, (2) the three-year allocation method, or (3) the inclusion of the adjustments in 2012 – 2015 income using the new accounting method as indicated above.

Loss carryovers. Net operating loss or capital loss Rev. Rul. 64-245carrybacks or carryovers that are allowed as a deduction for any tax year to which adjustments were allocated under the three-year allocation method or an allocation based on the new accounting method must be taken into account before computing the limits on tax under those allocation methods. In applying the two methods, the taxpayer must account for an increase or decrease in tax for any tax year preceding the year of change that is affected by the NOL or capital loss I.R.C. §481(b)(3)carryback or carryover, even if no adjustment was otherwise allocated to such year.

I.R.C. §481(B)(3)(B)Items dependent on income. If the treatment of any Treas. Reg. §1.481-2(C)(6)item depends on the amount of gross income, adjusted gross income, or taxable income, such as medical expenses, charitable contributions, and credits against tax, the item must be determined for purposes of each successive computation of the limit on the tax increase by accounting for the proper portion of the amount of any adjustments required by the accounting method change to be included in each computation.

Assessment of deficiency, refund barred. The increase or decrease in tax for any tax year for which an assessment of any deficiency, or a credit or refund of any overpayment, is prevented by any law or rule of law. computations must be based on the Treas. Reg. §1.481-2(C)(7)tax previously determined for such year.

Special rules for partnership and S corporation accounting method changes. The limits on tax with respect to adjustments required for a Treas. Reg. §1.481-2(C)(5)change in accounting methods by a partnership are computed using the partnership’s taxable income, but the limits are then applied to the taxable income of the individual partners. Each partner must account in the year of change for his distributive share of the partnership items as adjusted. The limits on the increase in tax are then applied to a partner whose taxable income was increased by more than $3,000 because of the adjustments to the partnership's taxable income. The partner need not have been a member of the partnership for the two tax years immediately preceding the year of change, nor have been a member for all the tax years affected by the computation of the limitation. S corporations and their individual shareholders are treated in a similar manner.
